Leukemia Diagnosis

In this case study, we take a look at patients from two different leukemia strains: Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) and Acute myelogenous leukemia (AML). As seen from the charts above, these cancerâ€™s still carry high mortality rates, and thus identifying patients by their correct subcategory of leukemia is critical in providing precision treatment.

Dataset

Our dataset consisted of 38 leukemia patients in our training set, 27 of whom had acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), and 11 of whom had acute myelogenous leukemia (AML). The data points included gene expression readouts across 7500 different genes, which are typically collected via blood sample for leukemia patients.

Decision Boundaries

Using the fact that gene measurements are heavily correlated, we begun by applying Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to our dataset, reducing the dimensionality of our data from 7500 to 3. We used two classification methods: Linear Regression and Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA), giving us linear and parabolic decision boundaries respectively.

Results

We validate our models on a test set of 34 patients, 20 of whom have ALL, and 14 of whom have AML. Our simpler model of linear regression gives us an accuracy rate of 85.2% (29 Patients correctly classified). Moving on to Quadratic Discriminant Analysis, we see a higher accuracy rate of 91.1% (31 Patients correctly classified). Therefore, we see that our models are able to perform with high accuracy, even when given limited training data.
